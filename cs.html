🏆 Internal Exam Questions

Is there a difference between Cybercrime & Cyber Fraud? Explain.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Definition of Cybercrime: Cybercrime is any illegal activity in which a computer, network, or internet-connected device is the tool, target, or place of the crime. It is a broad umbrella term covering hacking, DoS attacks, cyberstalking, spreading malware, and digital espionage.
  2. Definition of Cyber Fraud: Cyber fraud is a specific subset of cybercrime involving deliberate deception, misrepresentation, or trickery via digital means to obtain financial benefit, steal personal information, or cause harm. It always requires fraudulent intent.
  3. Scope and Relationship: All cyber frauds are cybercrimes, but not all cybercrimes are cyber frauds. Example: hacking a website for thrill is a cybercrime but not fraud; phishing to steal bank credentials is both.
  4. Motive and Intent: Cybercrime may be driven by curiosity, revenge, political ideology, financial greed, or vandalism. Cyber fraud is exclusively motivated by deception for personal or financial gain.
  5. Examples Compared:
    • Cybercrime only: DDoS attacks, spreading ransomware, hacking government systems, cyberstalking.
    • Cyber Fraud: Phishing emails, fake e-commerce sites, online banking fraud, identity theft for financial use, advance-fee scams (419 fraud).
  6. Legal Framework in India: Both are addressed under the IT Act 2000 (Sections 43, 66, 66C, 66D). Cyber fraud additionally invokes IPC Sections 415–420 (cheating & dishonest inducement), which carry additional penalties.
  7. Detection & Impact: Cybercrime may be detected quickly (e.g., a DDoS crash); cyber fraud can remain undetected for months. Fraud causes direct, quantifiable financial harm, while cybercrime can have broader systemic impacts.
How do we classify Cybercrimes? Explain each one briefly.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Crimes Against Individuals: Target a specific person — includes cyberstalking, online harassment, email bombing, defamation, morphing of images, and identity theft. The victim suffers personal, reputational, or psychological harm.
  2. Crimes Against Property: Involve theft or damage of digital or financial assets — credit card fraud, intellectual property theft, software piracy, unauthorized fund transfers, and virtual property theft in gaming environments.
  3. Crimes Against Organizations: Corporate espionage, ransomware attacks, insider data theft, DoS/DDoS, and sabotage of business systems. These cause financial, operational, and reputational damage to businesses.
  4. Crimes Against Society & Government: Cyber terrorism, attacks on critical national infrastructure (power grids, water supply, hospitals), hacking of government databases, and spreading anti-national propaganda. These threaten public order and national security.
  5. Content-Related Crimes: Creation, distribution, or possession of illegal digital content — child exploitation material, hate speech, obscene content. Covered under Section 67 of IT Act 2000.
  6. Financial / Economic Crimes: Online banking fraud, cryptocurrency theft, phishing, Ponzi schemes run via websites, e-commerce fraud, and money laundering through digital channels.
  7. Type I vs Type II: Type I are single-event technical crimes (installing malware, one-time hack). Type II are ongoing, relationship-based crimes (grooming, long-term cyberstalking, sustained social engineering campaigns).
What are the different types of Cybercriminals? / Who is a Cyber Criminal?
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Hackers (White / Black / Grey Hat): White-hat hackers (ethical hackers) test systems with authorization. Black-hat hackers exploit vulnerabilities illegally for personal gain or damage. Grey-hat hackers sometimes act without permission but without malicious intent — they may disclose vulnerabilities after exploiting them.
  2. Crackers: Break software protection mechanisms — crack software licenses, bypass DRM, or break authentication — to distribute pirated software or gain unauthorized access. More destructive and criminal than exploratory hackers.
  3. Script Kiddies: Inexperienced individuals who use pre-built tools, scripts, and exploit kits downloaded from the internet without deep technical knowledge. Motivated by thrill and notoriety, not specific goals. Despite low skill, they can cause significant damage.
  4. Phishers & Social Engineers: Highly skilled at human manipulation. Use deceptive emails, fake websites, and phone calls (vishing) to trick victims into revealing credentials, financial data, or confidential information.
  5. Insider Threats: Disgruntled employees, ex-employees, or contractors who misuse authorized access to steal data, sabotage systems, or leak confidential information — often motivated by revenge, financial gain, or coercion.
  6. Cyber Terrorists: Politically or ideologically motivated criminals who attack critical infrastructure, financial systems, or government agencies to create fear, disruption, or political leverage. Examples include attacks on power grids and military networks.
  7. State-Sponsored Hackers & Organized Crime Groups: Nation-state actors (APT groups) conduct large-scale espionage and sabotage. Criminal organizations run ransomware-as-a-service operations, large phishing campaigns, and dark web marketplaces, treating cybercrime as a business enterprise.
Discuss the evils and perils of Cyber Threats for Organizations.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Financial Losses: Data breaches, ransomware payments, fraud, and remediation costs run into millions. The global average cost of a data breach exceeds $4.45 million. Ransomware demands can paralyze organizations until paid.
  2. Reputation and Brand Damage: A single security breach destroys years of built customer trust, leading to client loss, negative media coverage, and lasting brand damage — often more costly than the incident itself.
  3. Operational Disruption: DDoS attacks, ransomware, and system compromises halt production, customer service, and transactions — violating SLAs, causing revenue loss, and jeopardizing business continuity.
  4. Intellectual Property (IP) Theft: Targeted corporate espionage steals trade secrets, R&D data, product formulas, and competitive strategies, handing rivals an unfair advantage and eliminating years of investment.
  5. Legal and Regulatory Consequences: Organizations that fail to protect data face GDPR fines (up to 4% of global annual turnover), lawsuits from affected parties, mandated security audits, and loss of operating licenses in regulated industries.
  6. Supply Chain Attacks: Attackers compromise trusted vendors to gain access to larger targets (SolarWinds 2020, compromised 18,000+ organizations). Organizations inherit risks from every third-party they integrate with.
  7. Employee and Customer Data Exposure: Breaches expose Personally Identifiable Information (PII), enabling secondary identity theft and fraud for employees and customers — multiplying the harm beyond the organization itself.

How do viruses get disseminated? Explain with diagrams.
6 MARKS
  1. Email Attachments: Viruses are embedded in file attachments (.exe, .doc with macros, .pdf exploits). When the recipient opens the attachment, the virus executes and infects the system, often silently in the background.
  2. Infected Websites – Drive-by Downloads: Visiting a malicious or compromised legitimate website triggers automatic silent download and execution of virus code, exploiting unpatched browser or plugin vulnerabilities.
  3. Removable Media (USB / External Drives): Autorun features execute virus code the moment an infected USB drive is plugged in. This is a common vector in air-gapped (offline) networks like industrial control systems.
  4. File Sharing Networks (P2P): Pirated software, games, and movies downloaded through peer-to-peer networks are frequently bundled with hidden virus executables that activate when the user runs the downloaded content.
  5. Social Engineering & Malicious Downloads: Victims are tricked via pop-ups, fake alerts, or phishing messages into voluntarily downloading and running infected software disguised as legitimate updates, codecs, or tools.
  6. Network Propagation: Once inside a network, a virus spreads laterally to connected machines via shared folders, open SMB ports, and unpatched vulnerabilities — enabling rapid enterprise-wide infection (e.g., WannaCry via EternalBlue).

Explain the concept of Information Security. Discuss its importance in protecting digital information.
6 MARKS
  1. Definition: Information Security (InfoSec) is the set of practices, technologies, and policies designed to protect digital and physical information from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, destruction, or disruption. It applies across all media — digital, physical, and human.
  2. CIA Triad — The Core Pillars:
    • Confidentiality: Information is accessible only to authorized individuals (enforced via encryption, access controls, authentication).
    • Integrity: Information remains accurate and unmodified by unauthorized parties (enforced via hashing, digital signatures, checksums).
    • Availability: Authorized users can access information and systems whenever needed (enforced via redundancy, backups, DDoS protection).
  3. Extended Principles: Authentication (verifying identity), Authorization (permission-based access), Non-repudiation (preventing denial of performed actions — digital signatures), and Accountability (audit trails).
  4. Types of InfoSec: Network security (firewalls, IDS/IPS), Application security (secure coding, OWASP practices), Data security (DLP, encryption), Physical security (server room controls), and Operational security (policies, procedures, training).
  5. Importance for Individuals: Protects personal data (bank records, health info, Aadhaar) from theft, misuse, identity fraud, and reputational harm.
  6. Importance for Organizations: Protects trade secrets, customer data, and financial systems. Ensures regulatory compliance (IT Act, GDPR), maintains business continuity, and builds customer trust.
  7. Importance for Governments: Safeguards classified military and diplomatic information, protects critical national infrastructure (power, water, transport), and defends against state-sponsored cyber espionage.
Discuss the legal perspective of Cybercrime in India with reference to provisions of IT Act 2000.
6 MARKS
  1. Background: India's Information Technology Act 2000 is the primary legislation governing cybercrimes, electronic records, and digital commerce. It was significantly amended in 2008 (IT Amendment Act) to address emerging cybercrime threats and align with global standards.
  2. Section 43 – Unauthorized Access / Computer Damage: Penalizes unauthorized access, data downloading without consent, introducing viruses, and disrupting computer systems. The guilty party is liable to pay damages up to ₹1 crore to the affected party.
  3. Section 66 – Computer Related Offences (Hacking): Any person who dishonestly or fraudulently commits acts under Section 43 faces imprisonment up to 3 years or a fine up to ₹5 lakhs, or both.
  4. Section 66C – Identity Theft: Fraudulent use of another's electronic signature, password, or unique identification feature is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment and a fine up to ₹1 lakh.
  5. Section 66D – Cheating by Personation Using Computer Resources: Impersonating another person online to cheat or deceive is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment and fine up to ₹1 lakh.
  6. Section 67 – Obscene Content Online: Publishing or transmitting obscene material electronically is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment (5 years on subsequent conviction) and fine up to ₹5 lakhs.
  7. Section 70 – Protected Systems: Unauthorized access to government-notified protected systems (critical infrastructure) carries imprisonment up to 10 years. Section 72 covers breach of confidentiality and privacy.
  8. Supporting Bodies: CERT-In (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team) monitors threats and coordinates response. State Cyber Crime Cells and the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) handle investigations and complaints.
Explain the major features and objectives of IT Act 2000.
6 MARKS

Objectives:

  1. Legal Recognition of Electronic Records & Signatures: Grants legal validity to digital documents and electronic/digital signatures, enabling paperless transactions in commerce, banking, and governance.
  2. Facilitate E-Commerce & E-Governance: Creates a trusted legal framework for online business, digital contracts, electronic payments, and government services accessible online — reducing friction and increasing inclusion.
  3. Prevent & Penalize Cybercrime: Defines specific cybercrime offences (hacking, identity theft, obscene content, denial of service) with clear penalties, providing a legal basis for prosecution.
  4. Data Protection: Section 43A requires organizations handling sensitive personal data to maintain "reasonable security practices." Non-compliance results in compensation liability to affected persons.

Key Features:

  1. Digital Signatures: Recognizes digital signatures (using public key cryptography) as legally equivalent to handwritten signatures, enabling secure and verifiable online authentication.
  2. Cyber Appellate Tribunal: Establishes a specialized tribunal for adjudicating cybercrime and IT disputes — providing faster, expert resolution compared to traditional civil courts.
  3. Liability of Network Service Providers (ISPs): Defines when intermediaries (ISPs, web hosts, social media platforms) are exempt from third-party content liability, provided they follow due diligence procedures.
  4. 2008 Amendment Enhancements: Added Sections 66A–66F (cybercrime offences), Section 43A (data protection), recognized electronic evidence more broadly, introduced cyber terrorism provisions (66F), and enhanced penalties for child exploitation content.
Describe the global perspective of Cybercrime. Explain how cybercrime has evolved worldwide.
6 MARKS
  1. Origins (1970s–80s): Cybercrime began with phone phreaking — manipulating telephone networks for free calls. The Morris Worm (1988) was one of the first internet-based cyberattacks, infecting ~6,000 computers and exposing networked system vulnerabilities.
  2. Internet Era (1990s): Commercialization of the internet brought website defacement, early online fraud, credit card theft, and rapid virus spread via email (ILOVEYOU worm, 1999 infected millions globally).
  3. Organized Cybercrime (2000s): Cybercrime transformed from solo acts to organized criminal enterprises. Botnets, professional phishing campaigns, ransomware, and dark web markets emerged with global professional criminal networks.
  4. Nation-State Cyber Warfare (2010s): Stuxnet (2010) — a sophisticated worm targeting Iranian nuclear centrifuges — marked a turning point where nation-states began using cyber weapons. The US, Russia, China, North Korea, and Iran are now recognized cyber powers.
  5. International Legal Framework: The Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001) is the first international treaty on cybercrime, facilitating cross-border cooperation. 65+ countries have ratified it. India participates as an observer but has not ratified it, creating extradition challenges.
  6. Ransomware & Supply Chain Era (2020s): WannaCry (2017) affected 150+ countries; SolarWinds attack (2020) compromised 18,000+ organizations including US government agencies. Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) democratized sophisticated attacks.
  7. Current Global Challenges: Cross-border jurisdiction gaps, anonymization tools (Tor, VPNs, cryptocurrency), AI-powered attacks, dark web marketplaces for stolen data, and inconsistent national laws make global cybercrime control extremely difficult.
What is the relationship between Cybercrime and Information Security? Explain with suitable examples.
6 MARKS
  1. Opposing Forces: Cybercrime and Information Security are directly opposed — cybercrime exploits weaknesses in information systems, while InfoSec works to identify, close, and monitor those weaknesses. Where security fails, crime thrives.
  2. CIA Triad Violations Enable Cybercrime: Confidentiality breaches lead to data theft; Integrity failures enable fraud and data manipulation; Availability disruptions are the goal of DoS/DDoS attacks. Each cybercrime maps to a CIA principle violation.
  3. Example — Weak Authentication → Account Hacking: An organization not enforcing multi-factor authentication (InfoSec gap) allows attackers to compromise accounts via credential stuffing or brute force (cybercrime).
  4. Example — Unencrypted Storage → Data Breach: Storing customer passwords in plaintext (InfoSec negligence) enables attackers who breach the database to steal and sell millions of credentials on dark web markets (cybercrime + cyber fraud).
  5. Example — Unpatched Systems → Ransomware: Organizations failing to apply security patches (InfoSec failure) are vulnerable to ransomware that exploits known vulnerabilities — the WannaCry attack exploited an unpatched Windows SMB vulnerability.
  6. Security as a Deterrent: Firewalls, IDS/IPS, encryption, security awareness training, and penetration testing directly reduce cybercrime opportunities by raising the cost and complexity of an attack.
  7. Continuous Arms Race: As security technologies improve, attackers innovate — zero-day exploits target unpatched vulnerabilities, social engineering bypasses technical controls, and AI is now used to craft highly convincing phishing emails. The relationship is a perpetually evolving conflict.
Explain the impact of Cybercrime on individuals, organisations, and governments.
6 MARKS
  1. Individuals – Financial Loss: Victims of phishing, online banking fraud, and credit card theft suffer direct monetary losses. Recovery is slow, and banks may not always reimburse losses from customer-initiated actions like clicking malicious links.
  2. Individuals – Identity Theft & Psychological Harm: Stolen PII is used to take loans, file false tax returns, or commit fraud in the victim's name — damaging credit scores and reputation. Cyberstalking causes chronic fear, anxiety, and psychological trauma.
  3. Organisations – Operational & Financial Impact: Ransomware halts operations entirely; data breaches require expensive forensic investigation, legal response, and customer notification. Average global data breach cost: $4.45 million (IBM 2023).
  4. Organisations – Reputation & Legal Liability: Post-breach customer attrition, class-action lawsuits, regulatory fines (GDPR up to 4% of global revenue), and mandatory security audits compound the damage well beyond the initial incident.
  5. Governments – National Security: Cyber espionage by foreign state actors leaks classified military and diplomatic secrets. Attacks on critical infrastructure (nuclear plants, power grids) pose existential national security risks.
  6. Governments – Political & Economic Disruption: Election interference through disinformation campaigns, attacks on financial markets, and disruption of government e-services destabilize democratic processes and economic confidence.
  7. Broader Societal Impact: Mass erosion of public trust in digital systems, increased government surveillance measures in response to threats, chilling effects on online free expression, and widened digital inequality as victims disengage from digital services.

📗 2-Mark Questions — Unit 1

Define Cybercrime [Internal]

Cybercrime is any criminal activity where a computer, network, or internet-connected device is the tool, target, or place of the crime. It includes hacking, phishing, identity theft, online fraud, and cyberstalking. In India, it is governed by the IT Act 2000.

Cyber Space

Cyberspace is the virtual environment formed by interconnected digital systems, computer networks, and the internet. It has no physical boundaries and serves as the domain where data is stored, transmitted, and processed globally, enabling communication and digital transactions.

Identity Theft

Identity theft is a cybercrime where a criminal steals personal information (Aadhaar, PAN, banking credentials) to impersonate the victim and commit fraud. It results in financial loss, credit damage, and significant psychological harm. Covered under Section 66C of IT Act 2000.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality is a core principle of information security ensuring that sensitive information is accessible only to authorized users. It is enforced through encryption, access controls, authentication, and need-to-know policies, preventing unauthorized disclosure.

Integrity

Integrity in information security ensures that data remains accurate, complete, and unaltered by unauthorized parties. It is maintained using hashing algorithms (SHA-256), digital signatures, and checksums that detect any unauthorized modifications to data in transit or at rest.

Availability

Availability ensures that information systems and data remain accessible to authorized users whenever required. It is maintained through redundancy, load balancing, regular backups, disaster recovery planning, and protection against DoS/DDoS attacks that aim to disrupt services.

Digital Evidence

Digital evidence is any electronically stored information used in a legal investigation or court proceeding — emails, chat logs, metadata, deleted files, server logs, or device data. It must be carefully preserved to maintain admissibility and chain-of-custody integrity.

Cyber Law

Cyber law is the legal framework governing the use of computers, the internet, and digital technologies. India's IT Act 2000 is the primary cyber law, addressing e-commerce, cybercrime definitions and penalties, digital signatures, and data protection obligations.

Cyber Offence

A cyber offence is any illegal act committed using computers, networks, or the internet. Examples include hacking, identity theft, malware distribution, cyberstalking, and online fraud. Cyber offences are prosecuted under the IT Act 2000 and relevant IPC sections in India.

Hacking

Hacking is unauthorized access to or manipulation of a computer system, network, or data. White-hat hackers operate with permission (ethical/security testing); black-hat hackers exploit vulnerabilities illegally for personal gain or damage. Punishable under Section 66, IT Act 2000.

Information Security

Information security is the discipline of protecting digital information from unauthorized access, disclosure, modification, and destruction. It is built on the CIA triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability — using tools like firewalls, encryption, and access control systems.

Cyber Crime & Cyber Criminal

Cybercrime: Any illegal activity using computers or networks as tool or target. Cyber Criminal: A person who commits cybercrime — may be a hacker, phisher, insider, or organized crime member. They exploit technical vulnerabilities or human psychology for unauthorized access or gain.

Two Types of Cyber Crime

1. Hacking: Unauthorized access to computer systems to steal, modify, or destroy data (Section 66, IT Act). 2. Phishing: Deceptive emails or fake websites that trick users into revealing passwords and financial details, enabling fraud (Section 66D, IT Act).

Two Challenges in Controlling Cyber Crime

1. Anonymity: Criminals use Tor, VPNs, and fake digital identities to hide their true location and identity, making tracking and attribution extremely difficult. 2. Cross-border Jurisdiction: Cybercriminals operate from foreign countries where local laws may not apply, complicating investigation, prosecution, and extradition.

Explain the difference between Passive & Active Attacks. Provide examples.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Definition – Passive Attack: A passive attack involves unauthorized interception or monitoring of data transmissions without altering or disrupting them. The attacker remains in "listen-only" mode, making these attacks very difficult to detect.
  2. Definition – Active Attack: An active attack involves deliberate modification, deletion, fabrication, or disruption of data or systems. The attacker interacts with the target, making these attacks more visible but also more immediately damaging.
  3. Detection: Passive attacks are nearly impossible to detect in real-time because no data is altered and no anomalies are generated. Active attacks are more detectable via intrusion detection systems, anomaly alerts, or visible service disruption.
  4. Intent and Damage: Passive attacks are reconnaissance-focused — gathering intelligence for future exploitation. Active attacks cause immediate, measurable harm: system disruption, data corruption, unauthorized access, or service denial.
  5. Prevention: Passive attacks are prevented via strong encryption (so intercepted data is useless). Active attacks require firewalls, IDS/IPS, authentication, rate limiting, and input validation to detect and block.

Examples Eavesdropping, Traffic Analysis, Sniffing DoS/DDoS, Replay, Masquerade, SQL Injection

  1. Examples – Passive: Network sniffing (Wireshark on unsecured Wi-Fi), traffic analysis to determine communication patterns, eavesdropping on unencrypted HTTP/FTP sessions, and email interception.
  2. Examples – Active: DoS/DDoS (flooding a server), Man-in-the-Middle with session hijacking, replay attacks (capturing and resending authentication packets), SQL injection, ransomware deployment, and ARP poisoning.
What are the different attacks launched with Attack Vectors? Explain.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Definition of Attack Vector: An attack vector is the pathway or method used by a cybercriminal to gain unauthorized access to a system or network. It is the route through which the attacker delivers malicious payload or exploits a vulnerability.
  2. Email Attack Vectors: Phishing emails with malicious links/attachments, spear-phishing (targeted), whaling (targeting executives). Malware is delivered as Office document macros, PDF exploits, or zip archive payloads.
  3. Web-Based Vectors: Drive-by downloads (malicious code injected into legitimate websites), SQL injection (manipulating database queries), Cross-Site Scripting / XSS (injecting scripts into web pages viewed by victims).
  4. Network Vectors: Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) attacks (intercepting communications between two parties), packet sniffing on unsecured networks, session hijacking (stealing active session tokens), and ARP cache poisoning to redirect traffic.
  5. Physical Vectors: USB drop attacks (infected drives left in public places for curious users to plug in), shoulder surfing (observing screen/keyboard input), and physical access to unlocked machines.
  6. Social Engineering Vectors: Pretexting, baiting (fake software downloads/free offers), vishing (voice phishing), and SMiShing (SMS phishing) — all exploit human trust rather than technical vulnerabilities.
  7. Wireless & Insider Vectors: Rogue Access Points / Evil Twin attacks (fake Wi-Fi hotspots), Bluetooth hijacking, and insider threats (employees abusing authorized access). Supply chain attacks compromise software/hardware before reaching the target organization.
Explain how Botnets can be used as a fuel to Cybercrime.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. What is a Botnet: A botnet is a network of internet-connected devices (computers, IoT devices, smartphones) infected with malware and controlled remotely by a cybercriminal (the "bot herder") via a Command & Control (C&C) server. Individual infected devices are called "bots" or "zombies."
  2. How Bots are Recruited: Devices become bots when users open malicious email attachments, visit infected websites, or connect infected USB drives. The malware installs silently, joining the device to the botnet without the owner's knowledge.
  3. DDoS Attacks (Primary Use): The bot herder commands thousands of bots simultaneously to flood a target server with traffic, overwhelming it and causing denial of service. Each bot contributes small traffic; the aggregate is devastating. Example: Mirai botnet (2016) disrupted major internet services using infected IoT devices.
  4. Spam Campaigns at Scale: Botnets send billions of spam or phishing emails per day. Using distributed sending (from many different IPs), they evade spam filters and blacklists far more effectively than single-server campaigns.
  5. Credential Stuffing & Brute Force: Botnets systematically try stolen username/password combinations across thousands of websites simultaneously — exploiting password reuse to compromise accounts at scale.
  6. Cryptocurrency Mining & Click Fraud: Bot herders use compromised devices' CPU/GPU power for unauthorized cryptocurrency mining ("cryptojacking") or generate fraudulent advertising clicks to steal ad revenue.
  7. Crime-as-a-Service: Established botnets are rented to other criminals on dark web marketplaces (Botnet-as-a-Service), enabling anyone to launch massive cyberattacks with no technical skill. This "democratization of cybercrime" is why botnets are considered the engine fueling the cybercrime ecosystem.
What is Cyberstalking? As per your understanding, is it a crime under the Indian IT Act?
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Definition of Cyberstalking: Cyberstalking is the repeated, deliberate use of electronic means — email, social media, messaging apps, GPS tracking, spyware — to harass, threaten, monitor, or intimidate a specific individual, causing fear or distress.
  2. Methods Used: Sending threatening/harassing emails and messages, monitoring social media activity, using spyware or stalkerware to track location, doxxing (publishing private information), impersonating the victim online, and enlisting others to harass the target (mob cyberstalking).
  3. Types: Intimate partner cyberstalking (ex-partners), workplace cyberstalking, celebrity stalking, and stranger stalking (often escalating from online harassment). Victims are often women, though anyone can be targeted.
  4. Impact on Victims: Severe psychological trauma, chronic anxiety, fear for physical safety, social isolation, damage to reputation and relationships, and in extreme cases, escalation to real-world physical violence.
  5. Is it a Crime Under the Indian IT Act? Section 66A of IT Act 2000 (which covered online harassment) was struck down by the Supreme Court in 2015 (Shreya Singhal v. Union of India) as unconstitutional for being overly broad. However, cyberstalking is still criminalized under:
  6. Current Legal Provisions:
    • Section 354D IPC: Specifically penalizes stalking (including via internet and electronic communication) with up to 3 years imprisonment on first conviction.
    • Section 67 IT Act: Covers transmitting obscene/threatening content electronically.
    • Section 72 IT Act: Addresses breach of confidentiality and privacy.
    • Section 509 IPC: Words or gestures intended to insult the modesty of a woman.
  7. Preventive Measures: Strict privacy settings on social media, blocking and reporting harassers, documenting evidence (screenshots, logs), filing FIRs at Cyber Crime Cells, and using the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in).
Explain Cloud Computing and Cybercrime.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Cloud Computing Overview: Cloud computing delivers IT services (servers, storage, databases, software) over the internet on a pay-as-you-go basis. Models: IaaS (Infrastructure), PaaS (Platform), SaaS (Software). Examples: AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Dropbox, Gmail.
  2. Misconfigurations and Data Breaches: The most common cloud security failure is misconfigured storage buckets (e.g., AWS S3 buckets set to public), exposing millions of sensitive records. Capital One breach (2019) exposed 100 million+ records due to a misconfigured AWS firewall.
  3. Criminals Exploiting Cloud Infrastructure: Attackers abuse cloud platforms to host malware, phishing websites, and Command & Control (C&C) servers for botnets — leveraging the cloud's high availability and legitimate-looking IP addresses to evade blacklisting.
  4. Shared Tenancy Risks: Multiple customers share the same physical infrastructure in the cloud. Vulnerabilities like side-channel attacks (e.g., Spectre/Meltdown CPU vulnerabilities) can potentially allow one tenant to access another's data.
  5. Insider Threats at Cloud Providers: Cloud provider employees with privileged access to infrastructure could potentially access or leak customer data. Cloud providers themselves have been targets of social engineering and insider attacks.
  6. Jurisdictional and Legal Challenges: Cloud data may be stored across multiple countries with different data protection laws. When cybercrime occurs involving cloud data, determining which country's law applies and who has jurisdiction over evidence is highly complex.
  7. Shared Responsibility Model Gap: Security in the cloud is shared between the provider (securing infrastructure) and the customer (securing data, access, and applications). Many organizations misunderstand this division, leaving critical security gaps that cybercriminals exploit.

Explain how cyber criminals plan cyber attacks. Discuss the stages involved in executing cyber attacks.
6 MARKS

Cyber attacks follow a structured methodology known as the Cyber Kill Chain (Lockheed Martin, 2011):

  1. Stage 1 – Reconnaissance: The attacker gathers intelligence about the target — scanning social media, LinkedIn, company websites, WHOIS records, and using tools like Maltego, Shodan, and theHarvester. Both passive (no direct contact) and active scanning are used.
  2. Stage 2 – Weaponization: The attacker creates or selects an exploit and packages it with a malicious payload (e.g., embedding a Trojan in a PDF, crafting a phishing email with a macro-enabled document, or developing a custom exploit for a discovered vulnerability).
  3. Stage 3 – Delivery: The weaponized payload is transmitted to the target — via spear-phishing email, malicious website link, USB drop, watering-hole attack, or supply chain compromise.
  4. Stage 4 – Exploitation: The malicious payload exploits a vulnerability in the target system — a software bug, misconfiguration, or human action (opening attachment). The attacker gains initial code execution on the target machine.
  5. Stage 5 – Installation: The attacker installs a backdoor, Remote Access Trojan (RAT), or rootkit to establish persistent access — ensuring they can return even if the initial exploit is patched or detected.
  6. Stage 6 – Command & Control (C2): The compromised machine connects to the attacker's C&C server, enabling the attacker to remotely control it, issue commands, exfiltrate data, or recruit it into a botnet.
  7. Stage 7 – Actions on Objectives: The attacker achieves their goal — data exfiltration, ransomware deployment, intellectual property theft, credential harvesting, or lateral movement deeper into the network.

What is Social Engineering? Explain the different social engineering techniques used by attackers.
6 MARKS
  1. Definition: Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It bypasses technical security controls by exploiting human emotions — trust, fear, urgency, curiosity, and greed.
  2. Phishing: Mass deceptive emails appearing to come from trusted sources (banks, tech companies) urging recipients to click malicious links or enter credentials on fake websites. Spear-phishing is personalized to a specific individual; whaling targets executives.
  3. Vishing (Voice Phishing): Attackers call victims pretending to be bank officials, IT support, or government agents, verbally extracting OTPs, passwords, or personal information. Very effective because of the real-time, human-to-human nature.
  4. Pretexting: The attacker fabricates a convincing backstory (pretext) to establish trust before requesting sensitive information. Example: Posing as an IT auditor needing password access for a system check.
  5. Baiting: Enticing victims with something appealing — infected USB drives labeled "Salary List Q3" left in parking lots, or fake free software downloads — that deliver malware when the bait is taken.
  6. Quid Pro Quo: Offering a service in exchange for information — example: calling employees offering free IT help/upgrades in exchange for their login credentials.
  7. Tailgating / Piggybacking: Physical social engineering — following an authorized person through a secure access point (key-card door) without authenticating independently, gaining unauthorized physical access to restricted areas.
Describe the role of Cyber Cafes in Cybercrime and security measures to prevent misuse.
6 MARKS
  1. Shared Public Terminals: Cyber cafes use shared computers accessed by multiple strangers. This creates significant security risks — malware, keyloggers, or browser history from previous users can compromise data entered by subsequent users.
  2. Keyloggers on Shared Systems: Criminals install hardware or software keyloggers on café computers to silently capture every keystroke — collecting banking credentials, email passwords, and personal information without any visible sign.
  3. Anonymous Access for Cybercrime: Cyber cafes have historically offered quasi-anonymous internet access. Criminals use them to send threatening emails, conduct phishing campaigns, access illegal content, or launch attacks — making attribution difficult.
  4. Lack of Proper Monitoring: Many cafés lack CCTV, user activity logging, or identity verification, making it difficult for law enforcement to trace criminal activities back to specific individuals.
  5. Illegal Content Access: Cyber cafes are used to access and distribute child exploitation material, pirated content, and material banned under Indian law, often facilitated by inadequate filtering systems.
  6. Security Measures – Technical: Install endpoint security (antivirus, anti-keylogger), disable USB ports, clear browser data and cookies after each session (sandboxed/kiosk mode), use deep-freeze or session-reset software, and block illegal content via filtering.
  7. Security Measures – Regulatory: Under Indian law, cyber café owners must maintain user registration logs (name, ID proof, time of use) and install government-approved monitoring software. Regular police inspection and CCTV installation at workstations are mandated in many states.
Explain the methods used by attackers to gather information before launching cyber attacks (Reconnaissance).
6 MARKS
  1. Passive Reconnaissance (OSINT): Gathering publicly available information without directly interacting with the target. Sources include: company websites, LinkedIn profiles, job postings (revealing tech stack), social media, news articles, and annual reports.
  2. WHOIS & DNS Lookups: WHOIS queries reveal domain registration details, administrative contacts, and IP address ranges. DNS enumeration discovers subdomains, mail servers (MX records), and network architecture without triggering alerts.
  3. Google Dorking: Advanced Google search operators (e.g., filetype:pdf site:target.com, intitle:"index of") to find exposed files, login portals, error messages, or sensitive documents accidentally indexed by search engines.
  4. Shodan (Internet of Things Search Engine): Shodan indexes internet-connected devices and their open ports/services. Attackers use it to find exposed servers, webcams, SCADA systems, and vulnerable services without directly scanning the target.
  5. Active Scanning — Network Mapping: Tools like Nmap perform port scanning and service version detection on target IP ranges to identify open ports, running services, and operating system fingerprints — directly interacting with the target (generates network traffic/logs).
  6. Social Engineering for Information: Calling employees pretending to be vendors, IT staff, or auditors to gather internal network details, employee names, email formats, and system configurations through conversation.
  7. Tools Commonly Used: Maltego (relationship mapping and OSINT), theHarvester (email/domain enumeration), Recon-ng (web reconnaissance framework), Netcraft (website history & technology), and LinkedIn/Facebook for employee profiling and targeting spear-phishing.
Explain the relationship between Botnets and Distributed Cyber Attacks.
6 MARKS
  1. Botnets as the Infrastructure for DDoS: A botnet provides the distributed army of compromised machines required to execute a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack. Without a botnet, generating enough traffic to overwhelm a modern server from a single source is practically impossible.
  2. How DDoS Works via Botnet: The bot herder issues a command to the C&C server, which relays the attack instruction to all bots simultaneously. Thousands or millions of bots flood the target with requests — HTTP floods, UDP floods, SYN floods — overwhelming its capacity to respond.
  3. Distributed Nature Defeats Simple Defenses: Because attack traffic originates from thousands of legitimate-looking IP addresses across multiple countries, simple IP blocking is ineffective. The distributed nature is what makes botnet-driven DDoS so powerful and difficult to mitigate.
  4. Amplification Attacks: Botnets are used in reflection/amplification DDoS attacks — sending spoofed requests to public servers (DNS, NTP, memcached) that send large responses to the victim's IP. Small botnet traffic generates massively amplified attack volume (up to 51,000x amplification with memcached).
  5. P2P Botnet Architecture: Modern botnets use peer-to-peer (P2P) C&C rather than centralized servers — making them resilient to takedown since there is no single point of failure. Taking down one node does not disable the botnet.
  6. Real-World Examples: Mirai Botnet (2016) used 600,000+ compromised IoT devices (cameras, routers) to launch a 1.1 Tbps DDoS attack against DNS provider Dyn, disrupting Twitter, Netflix, Reddit, and GitHub for hours. The 2007 Estonia attacks used botnets to target government and banking infrastructure.
  7. Mitigation: BGP blackholing, rate limiting, DDoS scrubbing centers (Cloudflare, Akamai), anycast diffusion, and ISP-level traffic filtering are the primary defenses against large-scale botnet DDoS attacks.

📗 2-Mark Questions — Unit 2

What is Social Engineering? [Internal]

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of humans into performing actions or revealing confidential information. It exploits trust, fear, urgency, and authority rather than technical vulnerabilities. Examples include phishing emails, vishing calls, and pretexting scenarios.

What is Cyberstalking? [Internal]

Cyberstalking is the repeated, deliberate use of electronic communication (emails, social media, tracking apps) to harass, monitor, or intimidate a specific person. It causes fear and psychological distress. In India, it is punishable under Section 354D IPC and relevant IT Act provisions.

What are Botnets? [Internal]

A botnet is a network of internet-connected devices infected with malware and remotely controlled by an attacker (bot herder) via a Command & Control server. Individual infected devices are called bots or zombies. Botnets are used for DDoS attacks, spam, credential theft, and cryptojacking.

What is a Virus? [Internal]

A computer virus is a self-replicating malicious program that attaches itself to legitimate files and spreads when those files are executed. It requires human action to trigger (opening an infected file). Viruses can corrupt data, delete files, or open backdoors, and spread via email, USB drives, and downloads.

Name two Information Gathering Tools [Internal]

1. Maltego: A graphical OSINT and link analysis tool that maps relationships between people, organizations, domains, and IP addresses using public data. 2. Nmap (Network Mapper): A network scanning tool used to discover hosts, open ports, running services, and OS versions on a target network.

Attack Vectors

Attack vectors are the pathways or methods through which a cybercriminal gains unauthorized access to a system. Common vectors include phishing emails, malicious websites, infected USB drives, vulnerable network ports, social engineering, and compromised third-party software.

Phishing

Phishing is a social engineering attack using deceptive emails, messages, or websites that impersonate trusted entities to trick users into revealing credentials, financial information, or clicking malicious links. Spear-phishing is targeted; whaling targets executives. Covered under Section 66D, IT Act 2000.

Cloud Computing (Security Context)

Cloud computing delivers IT resources over the internet (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS). Security concerns include misconfigured storage buckets exposing data, shared tenancy risks, jurisdictional ambiguity for digital evidence, and criminals using cloud platforms to host malware and C&C servers.

Spoofing

Spoofing is the act of disguising a communication (email, IP address, DNS response, caller ID) to appear as a trusted, legitimate source. Types include: IP spoofing (forging source IP), email spoofing (faking sender address), ARP spoofing (linking attacker's MAC to victim's IP), and DNS spoofing.

Malware

Malware (malicious software) is any software intentionally designed to cause damage, steal data, or gain unauthorized access to systems. Types include viruses, worms, Trojans, ransomware, spyware, adware, rootkits, and keyloggers. It spreads via email, downloads, infected media, and network exploits.

Spam Email

Spam is unsolicited bulk email sent to large numbers of recipients, typically for advertising, phishing, or malware distribution. Spam consumes network bandwidth and storage, carries phishing payloads, spreads viruses, and constitutes approximately 45–85% of all email traffic globally. Filtered by spam detection algorithms.

Password Cracking

Password cracking is the process of recovering passwords from stored or transmitted data using techniques like brute force (trying all combinations), dictionary attacks (using common word lists), rainbow table attacks (precomputed hash lookups), and hybrid attacks. Defenses: strong hashing (bcrypt), salting, MFA, and account lockout policies.

Zombie Computer

A zombie computer is a device compromised by malware and secretly controlled by a cybercriminal as part of a botnet. The owner is unaware. Zombies are used to send spam, launch DDoS attacks, mine cryptocurrency, or relay attack traffic — giving the attacker anonymity while the victim bears responsibility.

Distributed Attack (DDoS)

A Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack uses multiple compromised systems (botnet) to simultaneously flood a target server or network with traffic, exhausting its resources and rendering it unable to serve legitimate users. It is "distributed" because attack traffic originates from many geographic locations simultaneously.

Online Fraud

Online fraud involves any deceptive scheme conducted via the internet for financial gain — including e-commerce fraud (fake sellers), advance-fee scams, auction fraud, investment scams, romance scams, and insurance fraud. It exploits the anonymity and reach of the internet to victimize users across borders.

Pretexting in Social Engineering

Pretexting is a social engineering technique where the attacker fabricates a believable false scenario (pretext) to manipulate a target into revealing information or performing an action. Example: An attacker calls an employee claiming to be an IT auditor needing their password to "verify a system backup." Trust is established through the narrative.

Cyber Cafe Misuse

Cyber cafe misuse refers to the exploitation of shared public computer terminals for illegal activities — accessing illegal content, sending threatening emails, conducting financial fraud, installing keyloggers to steal subsequent users' data, or using café anonymity to evade law enforcement tracking of criminal activities.

Differentiate between WAPkitting & WAPjacking with suitable examples.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. WAP (Wireless Access Protocol): WAP was the early-generation protocol enabling mobile devices to access internet services. Though largely replaced by modern mobile web standards, WAP-related attack terminologies remain relevant in mobile security.
  2. WAPkitting – Definition: WAPkitting involves setting up a rogue/fake Wireless Access Point (Evil Twin attack) that mimics a legitimate network (same SSID/network name). Unsuspecting users connect to it instead of the real network, allowing the attacker to intercept all their traffic, steal credentials, and inject malicious content.
  3. WAPkitting – Example: An attacker sets up a fake "CafeWiFi_Free" hotspot in a coffee shop, identical in name to the café's real network. When customers connect, their banking passwords, emails, and messages are intercepted by the attacker via Man-in-the-Middle interception.
  4. WAPjacking – Definition: WAPjacking involves hijacking or compromising a legitimate, already-existing wireless access point — by exploiting weak passwords (WEP/WPA brute force), router vulnerabilities, or physical access to the router — to redirect users' traffic through the attacker's system.
  5. WAPjacking – Example: An attacker cracks the weak WPA2 password of a home router or exploits an unpatched router firmware vulnerability to gain admin access. They then redirect DNS queries, inject ads, intercept sessions, or perform phishing attacks on all connected devices without setting up a separate fake network.

Common Defenses Against Both: Always use a VPN on public/unknown networks (encrypts traffic even if intercepted), verify network authenticity before connecting, enable HTTPS everywhere, disable auto-connect to open networks, and keep router firmware updated with WPA3 encryption where available.

List and explain the different components of a Wireless Network.
INTERNAL 6 MARKS Also in Syllabus
  1. Wireless Clients (Stations): End-user devices such as laptops, smartphones, tablets, and IoT devices equipped with a Wireless Network Interface Card (WNIC). They connect to the network wirelessly using IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi) standards and are the primary users and potential vulnerabilities of the network.
  2. Wireless Access Point (WAP / AP): The central hub that enables wireless devices to connect to a wired network. It transmits and receives Wi-Fi signals, manages device authentication, assigns IP addresses (via DHCP), and bridges wireless and wired networks. Security of the AP is critical — it is the primary attack surface.
  3. Wireless Router: Combines the functions of a WAP, network switch, and IP router. It directs traffic between the local wireless network and the internet, manages NAT (Network Address Translation), and provides basic firewall functionality. Most home and small-office networks use a combined router/WAP unit.
  4. SSID (Service Set Identifier): The human-readable name of a wireless network (e.g., "HomeNetwork_5G"). Devices use the SSID to identify and connect to the correct network. Hiding the SSID provides minimal security; WPA3 encryption and strong passwords are the real defenses.
  5. Wireless Security Protocols:
    • WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy): Outdated, easily cracked — considered insecure.
    • WPA / WPA2 (Wi-Fi Protected Access): Uses AES-CCMP encryption; WPA2 is current standard for most networks.
    • WPA3: Latest protocol — improved encryption, protection against brute-force offline dictionary attacks, and forward secrecy. Recommended for modern deployments.
  6. Antenna: Directs and amplifies the wireless signal transmission from the AP. Omnidirectional antennas broadcast in all directions; directional antennas focus signals toward specific areas. Signal strength and direction affect coverage area and interception risk.
  7. Firewall / IDS/IPS: Network firewall filters incoming and outgoing wireless traffic based on rules; Intrusion Detection/Prevention Systems monitor wireless traffic for attack patterns (rogue APs, deauthentication floods, evil twin attacks) and alert or block suspicious activity.

Explain the growth and proliferation of mobile and wireless devices. Discuss their impact on Cybercrime.
6 MARKS
  1. Explosive Growth: The number of mobile devices globally surpassed 8 billion, exceeding the world's population. Smartphone penetration in India has grown from ~5% in 2010 to over 65% today, driven by affordable devices, cheap data (Jio effect), and digital services expansion.
  2. Proliferation of Wireless Connectivity: The rollout of 4G/5G networks, widespread public Wi-Fi hotspots, and Bluetooth-enabled IoT devices has created an always-connected environment where billions of endpoints transmit sensitive data constantly.
  3. Expanded Attack Surface: Every mobile device is a potential entry point for cybercrime. The proliferation means attackers have billions more targets — many running outdated software, connected to insecure networks, or lacking proper security configuration.
  4. Rise of Mobile-Specific Cybercrime: Mobile growth drove new crime categories: SMiShing (SMS phishing), mobile banking fraud (UPI fraud in India), vishing (voice phishing), malicious app distribution via sideloading, and SIM swapping attacks to bypass OTP authentication.
  5. BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) Risks: Employees using personal devices for work mix personal and corporate data. If a personal device is compromised, corporate networks and data are exposed — organizations have limited control over the security posture of personal devices.
  6. IoT Proliferation: Smart TVs, home assistants, routers, cameras, and wearables are often deployed with default passwords and no security updates. The Mirai botnet (2016) demonstrated how millions of poorly secured IoT devices can be recruited for massive DDoS attacks.
  7. Data Privacy Implications: Mobile devices continuously collect location data, biometrics, contacts, messages, and browsing history. If compromised or if apps abuse permissions, this vast personal data enables highly targeted fraud, stalking, and identity theft.
Explain the security challenges posed by mobile devices in modern computing environments.
6 MARKS
  1. Insecure Wi-Fi Connectivity: Mobile devices frequently connect to public Wi-Fi hotspots that lack encryption. Attackers on the same network can intercept unencrypted traffic (HTTP) via MitM attacks, capturing credentials and session tokens.
  2. Malicious and Unofficial Applications: Sideloading apps from unofficial sources (APK files, third-party stores) bypasses platform security checks. Even official app stores occasionally contain malicious apps that steal data, display fraudulent ads, or mine cryptocurrency.
  3. Lost or Stolen Devices: Mobile devices are physically small and easily lost or stolen. Without strong screen lock, encryption, and remote wipe capabilities, a stolen phone provides full access to emails, banking apps, contacts, and stored credentials.
  4. Outdated OS and Unpatched Vulnerabilities: Many users delay or are unable to apply OS security updates. Budget Android devices often receive no long-term support. Unpatched vulnerabilities are actively exploited — e.g., Stagefright vulnerability affected ~950 million Android devices.
  5. Excessive App Permissions: Many apps request unnecessary permissions (contacts, microphone, camera, location) beyond their functional needs. Malicious apps exploit excessive permissions to collect sensitive data for espionage or targeted advertising.
  6. SMS and Call Interception: SS7 (Signaling System No. 7) protocol vulnerabilities allow sophisticated attackers to intercept SMS messages and calls globally — bypassing SMS-based two-factor authentication and enabling account takeovers.
  7. Mobile Phishing & Social Engineering: The small screen size of mobile devices makes phishing URLs and fake UI elements harder to detect. Mobile users are more likely to click suspicious links, and browser security indicators (padlocks, full URLs) are often hidden or truncated on mobile browsers.
Discuss credit card frauds in the wireless and mobile computing era.
6 MARKS
  1. Card-Not-Present (CNP) Fraud: The most prevalent form — using stolen card details (number, CVV, expiry) to make online or telephone purchases where the physical card is not required. Mobile commerce and e-commerce growth has massively increased CNP fraud exposure.
  2. NFC / Contactless Skimming: Near-Field Communication (NFC) enabled contactless payment cards can theoretically be skimmed by specially equipped devices in close proximity. Attackers use NFC readers to capture card data from cards in pockets or bags in crowded places.
  3. Fake Mobile Payment Apps: Malicious apps disguised as banking or UPI apps (PhonePe, BHIM clones) capture card/UPI PIN details entered by users who mistake them for the legitimate app. India has seen numerous such fake UPI apps.
  4. Phishing for Card Data: Smishing (SMS phishing) messages claiming "Your card has been blocked — verify now" direct users to fake banking sites that capture complete card details. The mobile context (urgency, smaller screen) makes users more vulnerable.
  5. Man-in-the-Middle on Public Wi-Fi: When conducting payment transactions over unencrypted or compromised Wi-Fi, attackers intercept card data, CVVs, and OTPs. Even HTTPS can be compromised via SSL stripping attacks on unprotected networks.
  6. SIM Swap Attack: Attackers convince or bribe telecom employees to transfer the victim's mobile number to an attacker-controlled SIM. They then receive all OTPs and authentication calls, enabling complete takeover of banking and payment accounts linked to that phone number.
  7. Preventive Measures: Enable transaction alerts (SMS/email) for all card activity; use virtual card numbers for online purchases; never enter card details on unverified links; activate 3D Secure (OTP-based) authentication; regularly monitor statements; report unauthorized charges immediately; avoid financial transactions on public Wi-Fi.
Describe the various attacks on mobile phones and cellphones. Suggest preventive measures.
6 MARKS
  1. Mobile Malware: Malicious software (Trojans, spyware, ransomware, adware) specifically designed for mobile platforms. Android is more frequently targeted due to open sideloading. Example: Pegasus spyware — a zero-click spyware capable of accessing microphone, camera, messages, and location without any user interaction.
  2. SMiShing (SMS Phishing): Deceptive SMS messages with malicious links or false emergency claims ("Your bank account suspended — click here"). The mobile context increases click rates as users are more impulsive on small screens.
  3. Bluetooth Attacks: Bluejacking (sending unsolicited messages), Bluesnarfing (unauthorized access to data via Bluetooth), and Bluebugging (gaining full control of a phone via Bluetooth) exploit Bluetooth vulnerabilities when devices are set to discoverable mode.
  4. SIM Cloning / SIM Swap: SIM cloning duplicates a victim's SIM card to intercept calls and messages. SIM swapping socially engineers telecom providers to reassign a victim's number to an attacker's SIM, allowing account takeovers via OTP interception.
  5. Rogue Mobile Applications: Fake or trojanized apps distributed via unofficial stores or through phishing links install malware, steal credentials, enable remote access, or subscribe users to premium services without consent.
  6. Man-in-the-Middle on Wi-Fi: Attackers on the same network intercept mobile device traffic via ARP poisoning or evil twin attacks, capturing unencrypted communications including login sessions and payment data.
  7. Preventive Measures:
    • Install apps only from official stores (Play Store, App Store); review permissions before granting.
    • Keep OS and apps fully updated; enable automatic security updates.
    • Use strong screen lock (biometric + PIN); enable full device encryption.
    • Disable Bluetooth and NFC when not in use; never leave device in discoverable mode.
    • Use a trusted VPN on public Wi-Fi networks.
    • Enable remote tracking and wipe (Find My Device / Google Find My) for loss/theft scenarios.
Explain the authentication services used in Mobile Device Security.
6 MARKS
  1. PIN / Password Authentication: The most basic form — a numeric PIN (4–8 digits) or alphanumeric password protects device access. Longer, complex passwords significantly raise the cost of brute-force attacks. Recommended: minimum 6-digit PIN; alphanumeric passwords for high-security needs.
  2. Biometric Authentication – Fingerprint: Fingerprint scanners (capacitive or ultrasonic) compare stored fingerprint templates. Fast, convenient, and difficult to spoof physically. Widely adopted as the primary unlock mechanism on modern smartphones. Stored fingerprint data remains on-device (TEE — Trusted Execution Environment).
  3. Facial Recognition: Uses front camera and/or infrared 3D mapping (Face ID – Apple) to authenticate users by facial geometry. IR-based 3D face recognition (like Apple Face ID) is highly secure; 2D camera-based recognition is less secure and can be spoofed with photos.
  4. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Combines two or more authentication factors — something you know (PIN), something you have (device/OTP), something you are (biometric). Mobile MFA examples: Google Authenticator TOTP codes, SMS OTPs, hardware security keys via NFC/Bluetooth.
  5. OTP (One-Time Password) via SMS: A time-limited single-use code sent to the registered mobile number during login. Widely used for banking and app authentication in India (UPI). Vulnerable to SIM swap attacks and SS7 interception. Considered weaker than authenticator apps.
  6. Device Registration & Mobile Device Management (MDM): Organizations use MDM solutions (Microsoft Intune, VMware Workspace ONE) to register and authenticate corporate mobile devices — enforcing security policies, enabling remote wipe, and requiring corporate certificate-based authentication before granting network access.
  7. Behavioral Authentication: Emerging technique that continuously authenticates users based on behavioral patterns — typing rhythm, gait pattern, touchscreen pressure, and usage patterns — providing passive continuous authentication beyond the initial login, detecting if a different person picks up an unlocked device.
Explain the security implications of mobile devices for organisations.
6 MARKS
  1. BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) Risk: When employees use personal smartphones for work, organizations have limited control over device security posture. A personal device infected with consumer malware can serve as an entry point into corporate networks and data.
  2. Data Leakage Through Mobile Apps: Corporate email, documents, and credentials stored on mobile devices can be leaked if apps mishandle data, use insecure cloud sync, or if third-party apps have overly broad data access permissions.
  3. Unmanaged Device Diversity: Employees use a wide range of devices (iOS, Android — various versions, manufacturers), each with different security capabilities, update frequencies, and vulnerability profiles. Ensuring consistent security across this diversity is extremely challenging for IT teams.
  4. Mobile Network Interception: Employees accessing corporate systems via public Wi-Fi (hotels, airports, cafes) expose corporate data to potential interception. Corporate VPN usage on mobile is often inconsistent or bypassed by employees for convenience.
  5. Loss and Theft Exposure: If an employee's corporate-enabled device is lost or stolen without proper encryption and remote wipe capabilities, all corporate data, emails, and credentials stored on the device are immediately at risk of exposure.
  6. Shadow IT via Mobile: Employees increasingly install unapproved third-party productivity apps (cloud storage, messaging) on their devices and use them for corporate communications — creating data flows and storage outside the organization's visibility and control.
  7. Organizational Security Measures: Implement MDM/EMM solutions, enforce device encryption and screen lock policies, require VPN for corporate access, enable remote wipe for lost devices, adopt app whitelisting, segment corporate and personal data via containerization (e.g., Samsung Knox, Work Profile), and conduct regular employee security awareness training.
Explain the importance of organisational security policies in the mobile computing era.
6 MARKS
  1. Establishing a Security Baseline: A mobile security policy defines the minimum security standards all devices accessing corporate resources must meet — screen lock requirements, encryption, OS version minimums, and approved apps — ensuring consistent protection regardless of device type.
  2. BYOD Policy Framework: A clear BYOD policy defines which personal devices are permitted, what data may be accessed, what the organization can monitor or remotely wipe, and what happens on employment termination — protecting both employee privacy and corporate data simultaneously.
  3. VPN and Network Access Controls: Policies mandating VPN use for remote corporate access ensure all mobile traffic is encrypted in transit, even on untrusted public Wi-Fi. Conditional access policies can deny network access to non-compliant devices.
  4. App Installation and Usage Policy: Restricting installation to MDM-approved apps prevents malware introduction via shadow IT. Containerization policies separate work and personal data on the same device, limiting lateral data exposure.
  5. Incident Response Procedures: Policies define response procedures for lost/stolen devices (immediate remote wipe procedures), data breach protocols, and employee reporting obligations — minimizing the window between device loss and data exposure.
  6. Regular Training and Compliance: Security awareness training tailored to mobile threats (SMiShing, vishing, app permissions) ensures employees understand their role in security. Regular policy compliance audits via MDM dashboards identify non-compliant devices before they become incidents.
  7. Legal and Regulatory Compliance: Documented mobile security policies provide evidence of "reasonable security practices" required under India's IT Act Section 43A and international regulations like GDPR — protecting organizations from liability in the event of a mobile-related data breach.
Discuss security issues related to laptops and portable computing devices.
6 MARKS
  1. Physical Theft and Loss: Laptops are compact, valuable, and frequently transported — making them prime theft targets. A stolen unencrypted laptop provides immediate access to all stored files, emails, saved passwords, and corporate VPN credentials.
  2. Unencrypted Storage: Without full disk encryption (BitLocker, FileVault, LUKS), a stolen laptop's hard drive can be removed and directly read on another machine, bypassing all OS-level authentication and security controls.
  3. Insecure Public Network Usage: Remote workers frequently use laptops at coffee shops, airports, and hotels on public Wi-Fi — subjecting corporate communications to potential interception, rogue AP attacks, and session hijacking.
  4. Outdated Software and Unpatched Vulnerabilities: Remote workers who rarely connect to the corporate network may not receive timely security patches. Outdated OS and software create exploitable vulnerabilities that attackers actively scan for on public networks.
  5. Malware via Physical Media: USB ports on laptops are entry points for malware via infected drives, accessories (e.g., "rubber ducky" USB keystroke injection attacks), or malicious charging cables.
  6. Shoulder Surfing and Visual Eavesdropping: In public spaces, sensitive information displayed on a laptop screen (confidential documents, source code, client data) can be observed by nearby individuals without any technical intrusion.
  7. Preventive Measures:
    • Enable full disk encryption (BitLocker/FileVault) on all laptops.
    • Use strong BIOS/UEFI passwords and disable boot from external media.
    • Attach physical security cables (Kensington lock) in public environments.
    • Use privacy screen protectors in public places.
    • Mandate VPN for all remote corporate access.
    • Enable remote tracking and remote wipe capabilities via MDM/endpoint management.
Explain the trends and mobility and their impact on cyber security.
6 MARKS
  1. 5G Network Expansion: 5G's ultra-low latency and massive device connectivity (enabling millions of IoT connections per sq. km) dramatically expands the attack surface. 5G network slicing and virtualized infrastructure introduce new classes of network-level vulnerabilities.
  2. Work-From-Home (WFH) and Remote Work: Post-pandemic normalization of remote work has permanently moved corporate computing outside traditional network perimeters. VPN usage, cloud service adoption, and home network security became critical security battlegrounds.

Explain hacking Bluetooth.
6 MARKS
  1. Overview of Bluetooth Hacking: Bluetooth operates in the 2.4GHz ISM band with a typical range of 10m (Class 2) to 100m (Class 1). Because many users leave Bluetooth enabled and devices in discoverable mode, attackers within range can exploit various Bluetooth protocol vulnerabilities.
  2. Bluejacking: The simplest Bluetooth attack — sending unsolicited text messages, contact cards, or images to a nearby device via Bluetooth's Object Push Profile. Not destructive, but intrusive. Used for spam advertising or to psychologically manipulate targets.
  3. Bluesnarfing: Unauthorized access to data stored on a Bluetooth device — contacts, calendar, emails, messages, and stored photos — without the device owner's knowledge or consent. Exploits flaws in the Object Exchange (OBEX) protocol implementation. A more serious attack than Bluejacking.
  4. Bluebugging: The most severe Bluetooth attack — gaining full remote control over a victim's phone, including making calls, sending messages, reading data, and activating the microphone for eavesdropping. Exploits Bluetooth's AT command interface; allows the attacker to operate the phone as if they had it in hand.
  5. BlueBorne Attack: A critical 2017 vulnerability (CVE-2017-0781) affecting over 5 billion devices. BlueBorne required no user interaction and no pairing — an attacker could compromise devices just by being within Bluetooth range, spread like a worm between devices, and gain full device control.
  6. Bluetooth Relay and Man-in-the-Middle: Attackers use relay devices to extend the effective range of Bluetooth connections, enabling attacks from greater distances. MitM attacks intercept and manipulate data between paired devices during the connection establishment phase.
  7. Preventive Measures:
    • Turn off Bluetooth when not actively in use — this is the single most effective defense.
    • Set device to "non-discoverable" mode in public places.
    • Reject unexpected pairing requests from unknown devices.
    • Regularly update device firmware to patch Bluetooth stack vulnerabilities.
    • Avoid pairing with unknown or untrusted devices in public.
Discuss organisational security measures for handling mobile devices securely.
6 MARKS
  1. Mobile Device Management (MDM): Deploy MDM solutions (Microsoft Intune, VMware Workspace ONE) to centrally manage all corporate mobile devices — enforce encryption, screen lock, approved app lists, OS version requirements, and remotely wipe devices on loss or theft.
  2. Network Access Control (NAC): Only allow devices meeting the security policy baseline (compliant OS version, encryption enabled, no jailbreak/root) to connect to the corporate network. Non-compliant devices are quarantined or given limited access.
  3. Corporate VPN Mandate: Require VPN connection for all corporate data access from mobile devices — encrypting traffic even on public or untrusted networks and routing it through corporate security inspection points.
  4. Containerization / Work Profiles: Separate work and personal data using containerization technologies (Samsung Knox, Android Work Profile, Apple Managed Apps) — ensuring that even if personal apps are compromised, corporate data in the container remains protected and separately encrypted.
  5. App Whitelisting and Vetting: Maintain a list of approved applications for corporate use. Use MDM to prevent installation of unapproved apps on corporate devices. Conduct security vetting of all business apps before deployment.
  6. Security Awareness Training: Regular training on mobile-specific threats (SMiShing, QR code phishing, vishing, fake app stores) makes employees the first line of defense. Simulated phishing tests on mobile (SMS-based) measure and improve employee vigilance.
  7. Remote Wipe and Asset Tracking: All corporate devices must have remote wipe capability configured. GPS tracking of corporate devices enables recovery before data exposure. Establish a clear, tested lost-device response procedure with defined timelines for remote wipe activation.

📗 2-Mark Questions — Unit 3

Wireless Communications

Wireless communication transmits data between devices over electromagnetic waves without physical cables. Technologies include Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11), Bluetooth, NFC, 4G/5G cellular, and satellite. Security challenges arise from signal interception, rogue access points, and protocol vulnerabilities in wireless transmission.

Mobile Malware

Mobile malware is malicious software targeting smartphones and tablets — including mobile Trojans, banking malware, ransomware, spyware (e.g., Pegasus), adware, and SMiShing-delivered malware. Android is more frequently targeted due to open app distribution. Prevention: use official app stores, keep OS updated, use mobile antivirus.

Mobile Phishing

Mobile phishing uses SMS (SMiShing), messaging apps, email, and fake apps to trick mobile users into revealing credentials or clicking malicious links. The small screen size of mobile devices makes it harder to verify URLs and security indicators, making users more susceptible than on desktop browsers.

Mobile Device Management (MDM)

MDM is a software solution that allows organizations to remotely manage, monitor, and secure employee mobile devices. It enforces security policies (encryption, screen lock, app control), enables remote wipe for lost devices, manages software updates, and tracks device compliance — all from a central management console.

Bluetooth Attack

Bluetooth attacks exploit the Bluetooth protocol to access or compromise nearby devices. Types: Bluejacking (unsolicited messages), Bluesnarfing (unauthorized data access), Bluebugging (full device control via AT commands). Prevention: keep Bluetooth off when not needed; set device to non-discoverable.

Laptop Security

Laptop security involves protecting portable computers from theft, unauthorized access, and cyber threats. Key measures: full disk encryption (BitLocker/FileVault), strong BIOS password, VPN for remote access, Kensington security cable in public, privacy screen protector, automatic screen lock, and remote wipe capability.

Data Encryption in Mobile Devices

Data encryption on mobile devices scrambles stored data using cryptographic keys so it is unreadable without authentication. Full-device encryption (AES-256 on modern Android/iOS) protects all data on the device's storage. Even if a device is stolen and the storage physically removed, encrypted data remains unreadable without the decryption key.

Wireless Network Vulnerability

Wireless network vulnerabilities arise from weak or outdated security protocols (WEP is easily cracked), default router credentials, open/unsecured networks, rogue access points (evil twin attacks), deauthentication frame attacks (Wi-Fi jamming), and poor physical security of access points. WPA3 and strong unique passwords significantly reduce these risks.

Device Registration in Mobile Security

Device registration is the process of enrolling a mobile device with an organization's MDM system or a trusted service, binding the device to a specific user identity. It enables security policy enforcement, app management, and conditional access — only registered and compliant devices are granted access to corporate resources and networks.

Authentication Service in Mobile Security

Authentication services verify the identity of a user or device before granting access. In mobile security, these include PIN/password, biometrics (fingerprint, face recognition), OTP (one-time password via SMS or authenticator app), PKI certificate-based authentication, and behavioral biometrics — providing layers of identity verification for device access and app logins.

Credit Card Frauds in Mobile Commerce

Mobile commerce credit card fraud includes card-not-present (CNP) fraud in online shopping apps, fake banking/UPI apps capturing card details, SMiShing leading to phishing payment pages, NFC skimming of contactless cards, and SIM swap attacks intercepting OTPs for transaction authorization. Prevention: virtual card numbers, transaction alerts, 3D Secure, and VPN on public networks.

Vishing, Phishing & SMiShing

Phishing: Deceptive emails impersonating trusted entities to steal credentials. Vishing: Voice phishing — fraudulent phone calls (pretending to be banks, IT support, government) to extract sensitive information verbally. SMiShing: SMS-based phishing using text messages with malicious links or fake alerts targeting mobile users. All three exploit trust and urgency.
